# ICP — Export

## The mode
They manage outbound shipments to 15 to 40 countries, usually for an industrial, manufacturing, or specialist goods business. Their day is built around paperwork: commercial invoices, packing lists, certificates of origin, and customs declarations. They know which countries require what and they know that one wrong HS code on a shipment to Saudi Arabia is three days at the border. They are not looking for a platform. They are looking for the day when they stop dreading a regulatory update from a destination country they have shipped to twice.

## Who they are
- Job title(s): Export Manager, International Logistics Manager, Trade Compliance Manager, Supply Chain Manager, Export Coordinator
- Company size: £5m–£100m revenue, 50–500 export shipments per month
- Team size: 2–8 people in export documentation and logistics
- Tooling: ERP (SAP, NetSuite, or Dynamics) for order management, manual document preparation in Word or Excel templates, carrier portals for booking, possibly a standalone customs declaration tool
- Reporting line: Reports to Supply Chain Director or Operations Director; works closely with sales on delivery commitments and with finance on duty drawback claims

## Top 3 pains (in their words)
1. **"We prepare 6 to 8 documents per shipment by hand and one error holds the entire consignment"** — Commercial invoice, packing list, certificate of origin, EUR.1, phytosanitary certificate where required, and a customs entry declaration. Each one is typed or copy-pasted from the ERP. A wrong unit count on the packing list against the commercial invoice triggers a discrepancy query at customs that takes two days to resolve.
2. **"Regulatory changes catch us after the fact"** — Saudi Arabia updates its labelling requirement for a product category. Turkey introduces a new conformity certificate. The Export Manager finds out when a shipment is already in transit and the freight forwarder calls asking for a document that was not in the pack.
3. **"There is a gap between finishing the documentation and booking the carrier and errors fall into that gap"** — The documents are prepared in one workflow, then the team switches to a carrier portal to book the collection. Shipment details are re-entered. The weight on the carrier booking does not always match the weight on the customs declaration. The discrepancy is caught at clearance.

## Top 3 jobs-to-be-done
1. **When I am processing an export order, I want all required documents generated from the product data, HS codes, and destination country rules already in the system, so I stop preparing documents manually and stop introducing transcription errors.**
2. **When a destination country changes its import requirements, I want to know before the next shipment to that country leaves the warehouse, so I can update the documentation pack rather than fix a customs hold.**
3. **When I finish the documentation, I want the carrier booking to happen in the same workflow with the same shipment data, so there is no re-entry step and no weight or dimension discrepancy.**

## Conversion triggers (what makes them book a call)
1. A customs hold costs them a contract with a new international buyer who loses confidence in their logistics operation.
2. A post-Brexit regulatory update to EU-UK trade documentation creates a backlog that the team cannot clear with their current manual process.
3. They win a new export lane (a new country or a new product category) and the documentation requirements are different from anything they currently handle.

## Disqualifiers (what makes them bounce)
1. The platform handles carrier booking but not documentation. Half the problem is unsolved and they need both in one workflow.
2. HS code classification is manual or advisory rather than automated. They already know HS codes. They need the system to apply them without human intervention.
3. No coverage for their specific carrier (DHL Express, FedEx International, or a freight forwarder they are contracted with). If the carrier is not connected, they cannot use the platform for live shipments.
4. The regulatory update feature is a manual alert rather than a rules-engine update. They do not want emails. They want the documentation template to change automatically.

## Words they use vs. words they reject

|---|---|
| Customs declaration | Customs form |
| HS code | Product code |
| Certificate of origin | Origin document |
| Consignment | Shipment batch |
| Pre-clearance | Advance customs filing |
| EORI number | Export identifier |
| Duty drawback | Tax refund |
| Freight forwarder | Logistics partner |

| Tariff classification | Product category |

## Competitive context
Export managers at this level typically use a freight forwarder who handles documentation, supplemented by their own manual processes for direct carrier shipments. When they evaluate a platform, they look at CargoWise (heavy, freight-focused), customs declaration tools like CDS (HMRC's system, declaration-only), and integrated platforms like ITD Connexx. The Meridian Trade Co case study (documentation from 4 hours to 1 hour per shipment, customs holds down 90%) speaks directly to their daily experience.

## Primary conversion goal
Run the Export Documentation Savings Estimator for their top 5 destination countries and book a 30-minute compliance walkthrough.

## Voice notes for this ICP
- Anna Kovacs's quote ("We used to dread regulatory changes. Now the system handles them before we even know they've happened.") is the single strongest message for this audience.
- The dread of regulatory changes is real. Do not soften it. Name specific examples: post-Brexit EORI requirements, IOSS for EU B2C, Saudi SABER conformity.
- Documentation preparation time is the most credible ROI metric for this ICP. The Meridian case study (4 hours to 1 hour per shipment) is specific enough to be trustworthy.
- This buyer reads legal and regulatory language every day. Do not over-simplify. Write at the level of someone who knows what a EUR.1 movement certificate is.
- Pair every feature with a consequence. Not "auto-generates documents" but "the packing list and commercial invoice match exactly, because they come from the same source data."
