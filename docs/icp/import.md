# ICP — Import

## The mode
They are managing inbound shipments from 4 to 12 supplier countries, mostly in Asia and Eastern Europe, into the UK or EU. Their day is organised around clearance timelines and purchase order schedules. A clearance delay does not just cost a fee. It idles a warehouse, stalls a production line, or pushes an out-of-stock event. They know that their current duty cost estimates are wrong by somewhere between 10% and 20% and it is affecting pricing decisions they made six months ago.

## Who they are
- Job title(s): Operations Director, Import Manager, Supply Chain Manager, Procurement Manager, Head of Logistics
- Company size: £5m–£100m revenue, 50–500 import shipments per month
- Team size: 2–8 people in supply chain, purchasing, and ops
- Tooling: ERP (SAP, NetSuite, or Dynamics) for PO management, a customs broker for clearance, carrier portals or freight forwarder for booking, spreadsheets for landed cost calculation
- Reporting line: Reports to COO, CFO, or MD; works closely with finance on landed cost and with procurement on supplier terms

## Top 3 pains (in their words)
1. **"One in four of our shipments hits a customs delay and we never know it is coming until it is already happening"** — Incorrect tariff classifications or missing documentation stop a shipment at the border. The first sign is a call from the customs broker asking for a document that should have been filed before the goods left the origin country. By that point the delay is already 48 hours in.
2. **"We calculate landed costs in a spreadsheet and we know they are wrong but we have nothing better"** — Duty rates, VAT on imports, carrier surcharges, and customs broker fees are all estimated from the previous quarter's actuals. When a supplier changes their packing configuration or a carrier adds a fuel surcharge, the spreadsheet is out of date before it is used.
3. **"We cannot tell finance what a shipment actually costs until it clears customs"** — The CFO wants landed cost data for pricing reviews. The Import Manager can give them a number two weeks after the goods arrive. By then, the pricing decision has already been made using the old estimate.

## Top 3 jobs-to-be-done
1. **When a purchase order is confirmed, I want the tariff classification and duty calculation to run automatically, so I have an accurate landed cost before the goods leave the origin country, not after they clear customs.**
2. **When I am preparing documentation for an inbound container, I want pre-clearance documents generated from the PO data and HS codes in the system, so the shipment clears on arrival rather than sitting for two days waiting for paperwork.**
3. **When a shipment enters the clearance process, I want real-time status visibility rather than waiting for the customs broker to call, so I can alert the warehouse and production schedule before a delay becomes a crisis.**

## Conversion triggers (what makes them book a call)
1. A clearance delay causes an out-of-stock event that costs sales or idles a production line, and the CFO asks what they are doing to prevent the next one.
2. Post-Brexit import rules for EU-origin goods have changed and their current compliance process has not caught up.
3. A pricing review reveals that margin erosion on a product line is explained by duty costs they had not correctly modelled.

## Disqualifiers (what makes them bounce)
1. The platform focuses on outbound shipping. If it does not explicitly address inbound clearance and duty calculation, they assume it is not built for importers.
2. Duty calculation is an estimate rather than a calculation from the actual tariff schedule. "Approximate" is not good enough for a CFO who is using the number for pricing.
3. No integration with their customs broker or HMRC's CDS. They are not replacing their customs broker. They want the platform to work alongside the existing clearance process.
4. The pre-clearance feature only covers standard goods. If they import goods subject to additional controls (food, chemicals, plant-based materials) and the platform does not handle those, the coverage gap is too large.

## Competitive context
Import managers typically rely on their freight forwarder or customs broker to handle clearance, with no visibility into the process until it fails. When they evaluate a platform, they look at customs declaration tools (CDS, Descartes CustomsInfo), trade compliance platforms (Avalara, Thomson Reuters ONESOURCE), and multi-carrier platforms that extend into import compliance like ITD Connexx. The Northgate Imports case study (clearance delays down 60%, duty cost accuracy from 82% to 97%, clearance time from 3 days to 1 day) is the most direct evidence available.

## Primary conversion goal
Run the Landed Cost Calculator for their top 3 import origins, compare against their current estimates, and book a 30-minute import compliance review.

## Voice notes for this ICP
- David Park's quote ("Knowing the exact duty cost before goods arrive changed how we price everything. No more margin erosion from surprise charges.") is the highest-converting message for this ICP.
- Finance is always in the room for this buyer. Frame every benefit in terms finance cares about: landed cost accuracy, budget predictability, margin protection.
- The 82% to 97% duty cost accuracy improvement (Northgate case study) is the most specific credibility signal available. Use it.
- The fear is not just clearance delays. It is the downstream consequences: the warehouse sitting idle, the production line stopping, the out-of-stock event. Name the downstream.
- Post-Brexit vocabulary matters here. UK importers from EU suppliers live with rules-of-origin requirements and EORI complications daily. Using that vocabulary signals the platform understands their specific context.
